  • It sort of felt like this was a semester-long class being crammed into a quarter.
  • He can be a little scattered, allowing discussion to wander or take up too much time. He was still adjusting to the quarter system, so it felt like everything was a bit squished.
  • None

  • Didn't seem to have a very structured vision for the class - it felt like assorted topics in HCI with a focus on security.
  • Only two: he struggled at the start of the course with the length of his lectures. Now that you've had a run through of the class in the quarter system context, I'd strongly suggest changing those lectures which tended to be either too long, filled with content, or which the class didn't seem very involved in. Second, with regard to test answers--in many cases my classmates and I found ourselves losing points for answers which, though correct, didn't quite match Blase's full answer. This lead to partial (or no) credit on questions which, to me, were much more open-ended than they were graded. I'd suggest using a practice exam or explicitly giving instructions before exams so that this confusion doesn't arise, or, on the more lenient end, grading the exams more open-ended. In any case, more communication needs to happen so that your exams are more understandable for students, at least the first time they take one.

General Information:

What aspects of the course should be changed?

  • More "security," less "usability." It felt kinda like a psych class. That, or update the course description to be more true to the actual course.
  • Maybe jettison some of the material in order to have time to cover everything.
  • Make it more clear whether the class is about holding research studies, or the academic fields of usable security. It seems really difficult to do both, and made it confusing for students who expected one but not the other. If the academic side, could benefit from more in-depth information, because a lot of the stuff Blase covered in lecture were basic tech-interest info that CS-majors or minors would know already. Also, please do not ask students to sign up for meetings 48-72 hours in advance. Although we will make it work, I found it inconsiderate.
  • The homeworks. They were fine in principle, but my main objection to them was that we put a lot of work into them but never got any feedback. Literally the TA's just signed off on the homeworks as long as you did it, wrote enough, and/or included the right buzzwords, and gave you 95-100%. This sounds nice except for the fact that it led to some surprises when it came time to take the exams, since we not basically no meaningful feedback on homework. Maybe assign project groups at the beginning of the class, and have the groups do the homeworks together, so that the course staff will be able to take more time giving feedback on them.
  • Project and general assignment structure.
  • Minor things about lectures and grading, mentioned earlier.
  • A lot. It was a really unreasonable class mostly about survey design as opposed to usable security.
  • Less about research design, more about how to actually build usable system
  • I'd say the second midterm seemed mostly extraneous to me and that another take home midterm would have suited the materials of the class better. Also a clearer idea of what was expected of us wrt a pilot study would have been good
  • it'd be helpful for blase to be present at the progress presentations to give feedback
  • Maybe shorten the final project requirements a little
  • I wasn't a fan of the quizzes every class. I don't think they were a very good metric for understanding materials or even the papers themselves. Also I think the psets often felt like busy work.
  • Some reorganization of the presentations would be nice. It was Blase's first time teaching the class at UChicago, so curriculum didn't quite fit well with the quarter system. I'm sure this will be better next year though.
  • I liked the group project but its scheduling needs a little more adjustment. Some of the HW questions were sort of busy-workish (time consuming but not very engaging)
  • Reduce the size of the homeworks. Focus less on the study aspect of usable security and more on the hci implementation details.
  • Shorten down the psets, if possible make the group project simpler or at least have fewer deliverables. I would also really have appreciated getting more of a primer on security because, although this is NOT a security class, many of the topics are of course related to computer security and I was largely unfamiliar with the concepts since I hadn't been able to get into a security class previously.
